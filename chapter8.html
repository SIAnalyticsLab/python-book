1. Why Data Visualization Matters

Data analysis is not complete until someone can understand the results.

Numbers alone are often:

  • Hard to interpret
  • Easy to misunderstand
  • Difficult to compare

Visualization helps us:

  • See patterns
  • Identify trends
  • Spot outliers
  • Communicate findings clearly

Good visualization turns data into insight.

Bad visualization can mislead just as easily as bad data.

2. Visualization Is About Questions, Not Charts

Before choosing a chart, ask:

  • What question am I answering?
  • Am I comparing values?
  • Am I showing change over time?
  • Am I exploring relationships?

Charts are tools ‚Äî not decorations.

3. A Simple Visualization Taxonomy

Univariate Visualizations (One Variable)

  • Bar charts
  • Histograms
  • Box plots

Bivariate Visualizations (Two Variables)

  • Line charts
  • Scatter plots
  • Grouped bar charts

Multivariate Visualizations (More Than Two Variables)

  • Colored scatter plots
  • Faceted charts
  • Bubble charts

Plotly supports all of these interactively.

4. Why Plotly?

Plotly is a modern visualization library that creates:

  • Interactive charts
  • Hover tooltips
  • Zoomable views
  • Publication-ready visuals

Plotly charts:

  • Work in notebooks
  • Render in HTML
  • Integrate seamlessly with Quarto
  • Are ideal for dashboards

This is why SI Analytics uses Plotly extensively.

7. Univariate Visualization: Bar Charts

Question: How many observations fall into each category?

fig = px.bar(
    df,
    x="day",
    title="Number of Observations by Day"
)

fig.show()

Bar charts are excellent for categorical summaries.

8. Univariate Visualization: Histograms

Question: How are values distributed?

fig = px.histogram(
    df,
    x="total_bill",
    nbins=20,
    title="Distribution of Total Bills"
)

fig.show()

Histograms help you understand spread, skewness, and outliers.

9. Bivariate Visualization: Scatter Plots

Question: Is there a relationship between two numeric variables?

fig = px.scatter(
    df,
    x="total_bill",
    y="tip",
    title="Relationship Between Total Bill and Tip"
)

fig.show()

Hover over points to explore values and patterns.

10. Adding a Third Variable (Multivariate)

fig = px.scatter(
    df,
    x="total_bill",
    y="tip",
    color="day",
    title="Bill vs Tip by Day"
)

fig.show()

Now the chart answers whether tipping behavior varies by day.

11. Line Charts: Trends Over Time

malaria_url = "https://raw.githubusercontent.com/SIAnalyticsLab/30daychallengeguide/main/malaria.csv"
malaria = pd.read_csv(malaria_url)

fig = px.line(
    malaria,
    x="year",
    y="cases",
    color="country",
    title="Malaria Cases Over Time"
)

fig.show()

Line charts reveal trends, growth, and declines.

12. Choosing the Right Chart

Question Recommended Chart
Compare categories Bar chart
Show distribution Histogram
Show relationship Scatter plot
Show trend over time Line chart
Compare groups over time Colored line chart

When in doubt, simpler is better.

13. Customizing for Clarity

fig.update_layout(
    xaxis_title="Total Bill ($)",
    yaxis_title="Tip ($)",
    title="Tip vs Total Bill"
)

Always label axes, use descriptive titles, and avoid clutter.

  • ‚ùå Using the wrong chart type

Good visualizations:

  • Avoid misleading scales
  • Respect uncertainty
  • Support decision-making
